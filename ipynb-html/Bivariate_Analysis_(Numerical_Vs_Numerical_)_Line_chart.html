B. Create line chart

In[48]:
# Define the columns to plot

columns_to_plot_2 = [['Sales', 'Expenses'], ['Revenue', 'Profit'], ['Sales', 'Expenses', 'Revenue'], ['Profit', 'Market_Value', 'Revenue', 'Expenses']]
titles = ['Sales vs. Expenses', 'Revenue vs. Profit', 'Sales vs. Expenses vs. Revenue', 'Profit vs. Market Value vs. Revenue vs Expenses']
axes_positions = [(0, 0), (0, 1), (1, 0), (1, 1)]

# Define the figure and axes

fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 2, figsize=(12, 10))
fig.suptitle('Trend Analysis of Numerical Columns', fontsize=16)

# Iterate through the subplots and plot the corresponding columns

for i, cols in enumerate(columns_to_plot_2):
    ax = axes[axes_positions[i][0], axes_positions[i][1]] #get the axis
    for col in cols:
        sns.lineplot(x='Year', y=col, data=df, ax=ax, label=col, marker='o')
    ax.set_title(titles[i])
    ax.set_xlabel('Year')
    ax.set_ylabel('Amount')
    ax.legend()
    ax.grid(True)

# Adjust layout for better spacing

plt.tight_layout(rect=[0, 0.03, 1, 0.95])

# Show the plots

plt.show()

4. How to interpret line chart

Key Features to Look For

Shape/Trend:

  • Overall Direction: Is the line generally going upwards (positive trend), downwards (negative trend), or staying relatively flat (no trend)?
  • Linearity: Is the change happening at a constant rate (straight line), or is it curving?
  • Rate of Change: Is the line steep (rapid change) or gradual (slow change)? Look for sections where the line is steeper or flatter.
  • Patterns: Are there any repeating patterns, such as cycles or seasonality?

Center:

  • While line charts don't have a median like box plots, you can look at the average level of the data over the period shown. This gives you a sense of the typical value.

Spread/Variability:

  • Line charts show the range of values over time. A large vertical distance between the highest and lowest points on the line indicates high variability.
  • Look for periods where the line is more volatile (lots of up-and-down movement) or more stable (less movement).

Outliers:

  • Points that are significantly higher or lower than the general trend can be considered potential outliers. These may indicate unusual events or errors in the data.

Gaps:

  • Gaps in the line indicate missing data for those periods. Consider why the data might be missing and whether it affects your interpretation.

Correlation (when comparing multiple lines):

  • If you have multiple lines on the same chart, consider how they relate to each other. Do they move in the same direction (positive correlation), opposite directions (negative correlation), or independently?

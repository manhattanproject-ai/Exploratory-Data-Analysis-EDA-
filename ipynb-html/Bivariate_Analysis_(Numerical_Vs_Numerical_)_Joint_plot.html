4. How to interpret Joint plot¶

Key Features to Look For:

  1. Central Bivariate Plot (Relationship between the two variables):

Pattern/Shape:

  • Linear: Points (in a scatter plot or implied by the density in a hexbin or KDE plot) tend to follow a straight line.
    • Positive Linear: As one variable increases, the other tends to increase.
    • Negative Linear: As one variable increases, the other tends to decrease.
  • Non-linear: Points follow a curved pattern, indicating a more complex relationship.
  • Clustering: Data points form distinct groups or clusters.
  • No Clear Pattern: Points are scattered randomly, suggesting a weak or no linear relationship.
  • Center (of the relationship):
    • For scatter plots, visually assess the general central tendency of the point cloud.
    • For hexbin plots, the darkest hexagons indicate the areas of highest concentration.
    • For KDE plots, the innermost contours represent the highest density.
    • For regression plots, the regression line shows the central tendency of the linear relationship.
  • Spread (of the relationship):
    • A wide scattering of points (or a broad area of density in hexbin/KDE) indicates a weaker relationship or more variability.
    • Points tightly clustered around a line or a dense area suggest a stronger relationship.
  • Outliers (in the bivariate space): Points that are far away from the main cluster or pattern in the central plot might be bivariate outliers.
  • Gaps (in the bivariate space): Regions in the central plot with very few or no data points might indicate gaps or a lack of observations for those combinations of the two variables.
  1. Top Marginal Plot (Distribution of the X-axis Variable):

Shape:

  • Symmetric: The distribution is balanced around a central point (e.g., bell-shaped or normal).
  • Skewed:
    • Right-skewed (positive skew): The tail of the distribution extends to the right (higher values).
    • Left-skewed (negative skew): The tail of the distribution extends to the left (lower values).
  • Unimodal: One peak in the distribution.
  • Bimodal or Multimodal: Multiple peaks, suggesting subgroups within the data.
  • Center:
    • Look at the peak of the histogram or KDE to estimate the mode (most frequent value).
    • The central tendency can also be inferred visually.
  • Spread:
    • A wider histogram or KDE indicates a larger range and more variability in the x-axis variable.
    • A narrower histogram or KDE indicates a smaller range and less variability.
  • Outliers: In a histogram, isolated bars far from the main distribution might suggest outliers. The tails of a KDE can also hint at potential outliers.
  • Gaps: Empty bins in a histogram indicate gaps in the observed values of the x-axis variable.
  1. Right Marginal Plot (Distribution of the Y-axis Variable):

Interpret the shape, center, spread, outliers, and gaps in the distribution of the y-axis variable in the same way as described for the top marginal plot.

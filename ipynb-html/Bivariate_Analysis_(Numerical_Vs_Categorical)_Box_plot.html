# --- Box Plots for Numerical vs Categorical Variables ---

numerical_features = ['sepal_length', 'sepal_width', 'petal_length', 'petal_width']
categorical_feature = 'species'

num_plots = len(numerical_features)
rows = (num_plots + 2) // 3
cols = min(num_plots, 2)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(rows, cols, figsize=(15, 5 * rows))
fig.suptitle("Box Plots of Iris Features by Species", fontsize=16, y=1.02)

axes = axes.flatten()

for i, numerical_col in enumerate(numerical_features):
    if i < len(axes):
        ax = axes[i]
        sns.boxplot(x=categorical_feature, y=numerical_col, data=iris, ax=ax, palette="viridis")
        ax.set_title(f"{numerical_col} by {categorical_feature}")
        ax.set_xlabel(categorical_feature)
        ax.set_ylabel(numerical_col)

# Remove any unused subplots
if num_plots < len(axes):
    for j in range(num_plots, len(axes)):
        fig.delaxes(axes[j])

plt.tight_layout(rect=[0, 0.03, 1, 0.95])
plt.show()

Interpretation of the box plots

Interpreting Individual Box Plots:

  • Fixed Acidity by Quality: The median fixed acidity appears relatively stable across different quality ratings, with a slight increase at quality 8. The spread is also similar across the qualities. This suggests fixed acidity might not be a strong indicator of wine quality.
  • Volatile Acidity by Quality: There's a clear negative trend. As wine quality increases, the median volatile acidity tends to decrease. This is expected, as high volatile acidity (primarily acetic acid) is generally considered a fault in wine.
  • Citric Acid by Quality: The median citric acid content shows a positive trend with increasing quality, although it plateaus at higher quality levels. Citric acid can contribute positively to a wine's freshness and balance.
  • Residual Sugar by Quality: The relationship is less clear. While lower quality wines show a wider range of residual sugar, the median doesn't exhibit a consistent upward or downward trend with increasing quality. Some high-quality wines can be dry (low residual sugar), while others might have some sweetness.
  • Chlorides by Quality: Similar to volatile acidity, there's a negative trend. Higher quality wines tend to have lower median chloride content. High levels of chlorides can contribute to a salty taste, which is generally undesirable.
  • Free Sulfur Dioxide by Quality: The median free sulfur dioxide appears relatively stable across quality ratings, although the spread seems to decrease slightly at higher qualities. Free SO2 acts as an antioxidant and antimicrobial.
  • Total Sulfur Dioxide by Quality: The median total sulfur dioxide shows a slight negative trend with increasing quality. However, the relationship isn't very strong. Total SO2 includes both free and bound forms.
  • Density by Quality: The median density appears relatively stable across quality ratings, with a slight decrease at the highest quality (8). Density is related to alcohol content and sugar levels.
  • pH by Quality: The median pH shows a slight positive trend with increasing quality, meaning higher quality wines tend to be slightly less acidic (higher pH). However, the range of pH is quite broad across all quality levels.
  • Sulphates by Quality: There's a positive trend. Higher quality wines tend to have higher median sulphate content. Sulphates (in the form of potassium sulfate) can contribute to bitterness and astringency but are also important for flavor complexity.
  • Alcohol by Quality: There's a clear positive trend. As wine quality increases, the median alcohol content tends to be higher. Alcohol contributes to the body, mouthfeel, and aroma of wine.

In Summary:

These box plots suggest that several chemical features have a noticeable relationship with the perceived quality of wine:

  • Negative Correlation with Quality: Volatile acidity and chlorides. Positive Correlation with Quality: Citric acid, sulphates, and alcohol. Less Clear or Weak Correlation: Fixed acidity, residual sugar, free sulfur dioxide, total sulfur dioxide, and pH.
  • It's important to note that these are bivariate relationships, and wine quality is likely influenced by a complex interplay of all these factors and others. These plots provide valuable insights into potential indicators of wine quality.
